Why You Need A Personal Project In Your Coding Journey

No course will teach you what a project you actually care about will. Here's why.

I'll never forget the moment I finally understood state management in React. Not from a tutorial. Not from a YouTube video at 1.5x speed. It clicked during a personal project I'd been quietly working on in my spare time — no deadline, no grade, no one watching.

Something about that specific combination — a problem I actually cared about, in a codebase entirely my own — created the conditions for real understanding. And it made me realise that personal projects aren't just a nice addition to a coding journey. For me, they've been the most important part of it.

What a personal project actually is

It's yours. That's the whole definition. No brief, no boss, no artificial constraints designed to keep the problem simple enough to teach. Just you, a problem you want to solve, and the freedom to figure it out however you choose.

That freedom is uncomfortable at first. Tutorials feel safer — someone has already solved the hard problems and is guiding you through them. A personal project offers no such comfort. But that discomfort is exactly where the learning happens. The moment you can't look up the answer because nobody has written about your specific problem in your specific context — that's the moment you become a developer rather than a student.

Why it beats courses and tutorials

Tutorials
  • Simplified, clean examples
  • Problems already solved for you
  • Someone else's decisions
  • Finished when the video ends
Personal project
  • Real, messy problems
  • You figure it out yourself
  • Every decision is yours
  • Never really finished — always growing

Tutorials teach you what other people already know. A personal project forces you to find out what you know — and more importantly, how you think when nobody is showing you the way.

It becomes your story

When I look back on my development as a programmer, I don't remember the courses. I remember the projects. The one that broke in a way I couldn't explain for two weeks. The one where I refactored the whole thing halfway through because I finally understood a better approach. The one that actually shipped and that people used.

Those projects are a record of how I think. They show persistence and creativity and the specific flavour of problems I'm drawn to. A CV lists your skills. A personal project shows them. It's the difference between saying you can swim and someone watching you swim.

The writing connection

I'm a writer as well as a developer, and the longer I do both, the more I see them as the same discipline in different clothes. A good story and a good codebase share the same qualities: clarity, structure, economy of expression, and a clear sense of what they're trying to do.

"When you build a personal project, you're not just solving a problem. You're telling a story through code — one that reflects how you see the world and how you approach hard things."

That's why personal projects matter beyond the technical skills they build. They give you something to say. And in a field where everyone has roughly similar qualifications, having something genuine to say is the thing that actually sets you apart.

Starting is the only hard part

The most common reason people don't have a personal project is that they're waiting until they're good enough to build the thing they actually want to build. This is a trap. You get good enough by building things, not by waiting.

Start smaller than you think you need to. Build something embarrassingly simple. The version 1 of anything just needs to exist — it doesn't need to be impressive. You can make it impressive in version 2, once you understand the problem well enough to solve it properly.

The project that teaches you the most will be the one you start today, not the one you plan perfectly and never begin.
